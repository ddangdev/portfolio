when we started building the poke bar feature, the goal was almost embarrassingly small: *make the menu feel like a real ordering flow, not a static gallery*. it was supposed to be a polish pass, two days of work, then back to the more important stuff.

that didn't happen. the poke bar quietly became the most-mentioned piece of the portfolio, the thing prospective clients open first when they want to "see what you can build." which raises a question worth thinking about: what made this small thing punch so far above its weight?

## the build wasn't the hard part

the actual code is unremarkable — a grid of items, a select state, a running total, a checkout. things any front-end engineer ships in a sprint. the magic, if you want to call it that, is in the *specificity*. it doesn't try to be a generic "ordering interface." it's a poke bar. you build a bowl. the items are real items. the receipt prints out like a real receipt.

> you don't have to be the most impressive thing on the internet. you have to be the most *specific* thing for the person looking right now.

specificity is what made it click. a restaurant owner sees the poke bar and immediately gets it: "that's how my customers would order." a recruiter sees it and thinks "this person ships things that feel real." the abstraction barrier — the gap between "i can imagine this for my business" and "i actually see it" — collapses to nothing.

## what changed when we added loyalty codes

the original v1 was just the bowl + checkout. clean enough. but it felt like a demo. then we added the 6-character loyalty code field with the 10% off path, and suddenly the whole thing felt **operational**. you could imagine the back office. you could imagine the rules. you could imagine getting a code text-blasted from your favorite spot.

the lesson worth remembering: the small details that make something feel *used* are usually the difference between a portfolio piece that gets mentioned and one that gets scrolled past. it's never the headline feature. it's always the quiet field nobody asked you to add.
