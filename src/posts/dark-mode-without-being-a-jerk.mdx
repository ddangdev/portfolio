---

i used to feel weird about it. phone out, hovering over the plate, angling for the light while my friends are already eating. but i kept doing it anyway, and now i have three years of restaurant meals documented in my camera roll.

## it's not for instagram

i almost never post these. they're for me. when a client says "i want my menu to feel like *this*" and waves vaguely at the air, i can scroll back to a meal i had at a place that felt exactly like what they're describing and show them.

> the best design references aren't on dribbble. they're in your camera roll from dinner last thursday.

## what i actually notice

i started paying attention to things i never would have otherwise:

- how the good places always serve food on plates that are slightly too big (negative space matters)
- how lighting changes what food looks like more than plating does
- how the best restaurant websites use the *exact same warm tones* as their dining rooms

## the habit that stuck

three years in, i have over 2,000 meal photos. not all good. most aren't. but when i'm designing a restaurant's website and i need to understand what makes a place feel warm, i open my camera roll before i open figma.

it's the cheapest, most useful design research i've ever done. and dinner's tax-deductible if you squint.
